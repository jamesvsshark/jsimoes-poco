Most systems fail because they optimize for technical elegance before operational reality.

## The real problem

Teams often think they have a tooling issue. In practice, they have a modeling issue.

The system reflects how engineers group code, not how operators execute work under constraints.

## Workflow mismatch patterns

### Missing handoff states

Real work includes waiting, review, rework, and escalation. Systems that skip these states force teams to invent side channels.

### Wrong unit of work

If the system tracks tasks but the business runs on jobs, batches, or exceptions, every dashboard will look clean while operations stay messy.

### No ownership boundaries

When responsibility is implicit, every delay becomes "someone else is handling it." At scale, this destroys throughput.

## Real-world design constraints

In real workflows, latency tolerance differs by step. Some transitions can batch overnight; others need immediate response.

Design should reflect that reality with asynchronous and synchronous paths chosen intentionally, not by framework defaults.

At scale, good systems make constraints visible: blocked reasons, queue age, retry counts, and SLA risk.

## Tradeoffs worth making

You usually trade local convenience for global consistency. That tradeoff is worthwhile in multi-team operations, where hidden inconsistency is more expensive than extra clicks.

## Takeaway

Systems work when they model work as it happens, not as we wish it happened.
