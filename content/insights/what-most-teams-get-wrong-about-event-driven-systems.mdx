title: "What most teams get wrong about event-driven systems"
description: "Event-driven architecture solves coupling problems, but introduces coordination problems."

---

Event-driven systems do not remove complexity. They move complexity into coordination.

## The real problem

Teams usually think messaging is about throughput and decoupling. That is only half of the story.

The actual challenge is designing for correctness under retries, reordering, consumer lag, and partial outages.

## Design flaws

### Events without ownership

When no team owns event definitions as contracts, topics drift. Producers evolve payloads casually, consumers compensate with fragile parsing, and reliability erodes over time.

### Conflating domain events with integration events

Not every internal state change should be broadcast externally. Mixing these concerns creates noisy streams and accidental coupling across services.

### Ignoring idempotency

In production systems, duplicate delivery is normal behavior. Systems that treat duplicates as rare edge cases eventually create financial or operational inconsistencies.

## Failure modes at scale

At scale, the dangerous failures are slow failures: consumer lag, poisoned messages, dead-letter backlogs, and stale derived views that look healthy from the outside.

Observability must include end-to-end lag, per-consumer error rates, replay volume, and contract version adoption. Topic-level metrics are not enough.

## Real constraints and tradeoffs

Ordering guarantees cost throughput. Fine-grained partitioning improves parallelism but complicates debugging and replay strategy. Long retention helps recovery but increases operational burden.

In real systems, the right answer is contextual: optimize for recoverability first, then optimize for speed.

## Takeaway

Event-driven architecture is not a transport choice. It is a data coordination model. If you do not design for failure and replay from day one, the system will eventually design it for you.
